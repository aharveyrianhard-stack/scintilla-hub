51.1%
Cents of profit (before depreciation) kept from each revenue dollar. Default = NVDA's historical 51.1%.
15.1%
What NVDA actually paid TTM (15.1%) — the EFFECTIVE rate, not the 21% US statutory rate.

Fixed from history (no sliders): D&A 3.06%, capex 3.49%, NWC 22.7% of revenue. NWC (net working capital) = receivables + inventory − payables — cash tied up just running the business. When revenue grows, NWC grows with it, silently eating ~22.7¢ of every NEW revenue dollar of free cash flow.

B · DISCOUNT RATE — CAPM → WACC, what a dollar tomorrow is worth today

Cost of Equity = Risk-free + β × Market-risk-premium; WACC blends it with after-tax cost of debt by market-value weights.

4.28%
The zero-risk baseline. FMP defaults to the US 10-Year Treasury yield on pull date (4.28%). We can also source this live ourselves via FMP's treasury-rates endpoint.
2.21
How violently the stock swings vs the market. NVDA 2.21 = over twice the market's moves → investors demand extra return. "Regression" in plain words: fit a line through ~5 years of the stock's weekly moves vs the S&P's moves; the slope is beta. Purely backward-looking. The three options: FMP raw = exactly that history. Blume-adjusted = 0.67×raw + 0.33 — because extreme betas empirically fade toward 1.0 as companies mature ("drift"), so raw beta OVERSTATES future risk for high-beta names; Blume is the forecast correction, not a different measurement. Peer median = the comps set's middle beta (TSM/AVGO/MU/AMD/INTC → 2.14) — "price NVDA's risk like its industry's." None is 'right': raw = what happened, Blume = best single forecast, peer = industry anchor.
4.72%
Extra return stocks as an asset class must pay over Treasuries. ⚠️ FMP sends this RAW: a fixed house constant (4.72%, same for every ticker), no underlying inputs exposed. The proper source is external: Damodaran (NYU), monthly, free — July 2026: 4.45% US, 4.17% mature markets. The sub-components inside an implied ERP (his machine, all observable — these become our four dials when we build our own module): ① today's S&P 500 index level · ② expected cash returned to shareholders (dividends + buybacks, next 12m and growing) · ③ analyst growth expectations for ~5 years · ④ terminal growth = the 10Y treasury. Solve for the discount rate that makes those cash flows worth today's index level → that's the market's expected return (right now ≈ 8.7% = 4.28% rf + 4.45% ERP); subtract the 10Y → ERP. So when stocks rally with no cash-flow change, implied ERP FALLS — it's a live market price of risk, not a constant.
▾ BUILD THE MRP YOURSELF — implied ERP from its subcomponents (live)
MRP = ERP = "equity risk premium in excess of the risk-free rate" — all the same thing: the extra annual return investors demand for holding stocks instead of Treasuries. FMP hides how it's built; here are the parts as dials. The model discounts the S&P 500's expected cash flows back to today's index level and solves for the return the market is pricing; ERP = that return − risk-free. Then a country-risk premium is added on top, weighted by where THIS company earns revenue (Damodaran's method — FMP gives us the geography). Change any part → recomputes → "Use →" pushes it into CAPM above.
7457.7
The price level of the S&P 500 index itself (^GSPC is just its ticker symbol) — today ≈7,460, live from FMP quote. It's the "price" the whole model solves against: higher index for the same cash flows → lower implied ERP (you're paying more for the same future dollars).
1.30%
Cash paid as dividends this year ÷ index level (~1.3%). Source: S&P / FMP index fundamentals.
2.20%
Value of shares companies BUY BACK per year ÷ index (~2.2%). Buybacks return cash to owners just like dividends — for the S&P they're the bigger half, and most naive models wrongly ignore them.
8.50%
How fast analysts expect total S&P earnings/cash flows to GROW per year over the next 5 years (bottom-up consensus). The big judgment call — nudge it and watch ERP swing.
4.28%
Growth after year 5, forever. Pinned to the 10Y Treasury by convention — nothing outgrows the economy indefinitely.
0.20%
Extra premium for earning revenue in riskier countries. NVDA FY26 mix (FMP geography): US 69% (0 premium), Taiwan 20%, China 9% → blended ≈0.20%. For an emerging-markets-heavy company this is LARGE. Damodaran publishes per-country premiums; we weight by FMP revenue-by-geography.

🔍 The cliff is the point: counts climb into the near years (40 analysts on FY28 revenue), collapse going out (7 on FY31 EPS), and hit ZERO at FY32 — the blank columns are real, not decoration. FY22–FY25 are historical consensus snapshots (what the Street expected then — beat/miss checks come from comparing them to actuals). FMP publishes counts for revenue and EPS only; middle lines ride between those two. Quarterly (period=quarter): same six lines, ~10 forward quarters, 11–12 analysts each.
📊 Line-item depth strategy (per Alan): use the furthest-down line the estimates support, fall back up the statement as coverage thins. For NVDA all five lines exist every year, but the CONFIDENCE thins — EPS analysts drop 30→7 by FY2031 while revenue keeps 16. A consensus-based FCF proxy = EBITDA − capex − cash taxes; capex has no consensus line here, so it gets bridged from historicals (that's the "gap fill from us"). Quarterly estimates exist for near periods; annual carries the long horizon. Agreed base methodology: take the consensus TOP LINE (revenue — the most analysts, the longest horizon) and CALCULATE the bottom line ourselves via margins — using deeper consensus lines (EBITDA, net income) as cross-checks where coverage is thick enough. Paid estimate feeds (Zacks/Estimize, low hundreds/mo) logged for later — not now.
